About

Three - it's a magic number.


Since the cocktail renaissance began around the turn of the twenty-first century, detractors have been complaining that too many of the new drinks have been turned out by mixologists that contain a surfeit of ingredients. "So what?" I often thought. Except, those detractors had a point.


Complexity may lead to the flattery of the senses, but not to imitation. If the Manhattan had eight ingredients, nobody would be making it today. There is practicality in the three-ingredient cocktail. Whisky, vermouth, bitters? You got this.


There's also honesty in the three-ingredient cocktail. When you get past five ingredients in a drink, the further additives are often there, not to add what's already present in the glass, but to correct what's still lacking in the mixture. If you spot a drink on a menu with seven or eight things in it, chances are two of those are attempting to mend a broken cocktail. That's not possible with a trio. There are no obfuscating ingredients which might cloud the drink or mask some unsoundness of the drink. Every player must be strong. As bartender Audrey Saunders once said, "The three-ingredient cocktail dosen't lie."


The three-ingredient cocktail has history on its side. Every time the cocktail world has stirred up dust within the great culture, it's because some three-legged liquid mixture has ventured onto the world's Main Street: the Mint Julep, Manhattan, Martini, Whiskey Sour, Margarita, and Moscow Mule - all three-ingredient game-changers.


This is all a lot of words to justify something that dosen't really need justiying. Most everyone agrees there is some virtue in simplicity. In the field of cocktails, it's possible to make a fairly strong argument for any style or method you choose to get behind.


While most of the cocktails that are below are well established are were invented decades ago, there are a number of new drinks. Finding them are more difficult, simply because solid, three-ingredient cocktail today are a tall order. Still, if I find any of them, I will attempt to add them here.

Improved Cocktails

"Improved cocktails" is a phrase coined in the mid-nineteenth century that has been resurrected in recent years. It's a fairly vague term meaning any old-style cocktail to which an enhancement or bit of flair has been applied, be it a dash of this or that ingredient or a twist of lemons.

Syrups:

  1. Simple Syrup (1:1)

    Heat the sugar and water in a saucepan over medium heat, stirring occasionally, until the sugar has dissolved. Remove from the heat, let cool, and then refrigerate. Stored in a tightly sealed container, the syrup keeps for a week.

    • 1 cup sugar
    • 1 cup water

  2. Rich Simple Syrup (2:1)

    Heat the sugar and water in a saucepan over medium heat, stirring occasionally, until the sugar has dissolved. Remove from the heat, let cool, and then refrigerate. Stored in a tightly sealed container, the syrup keeps for a week.

    • 1 cup sugar
    • 1/2 cup water

  3. Honey Syrup (1:1)

    Heat the honey and water in a saucepan over medium heat, stirring occasionally, until the ingredients have integrated. Remove from the heat, let cool, and then refrigerate. Stored in a tightly sealed container, the syrup keeps for a week.

    • 1 cup honey
    • 1 cup water

  4. Rich Honey Syrup (3:1)

    Heat the honey and water in a saucepan over medium heat, stirring occasionally, until the ingredients have integrated. Remove from the heat, let cool, and then refrigerate. Stored in a tightly sealed container, the syrup keeps for a week.

    • 1 cup honey
    • 1/3 cup water

  5. Lavender Simple Syrup (1:1)

    Heat the sugar and water in a saucepan over medium heat, stirring occasionally, until the ingredients have integrated. Remove from the heat, gently crush the lavender leaves and add them to the sugar water. Stir, then let sit at room temperature for at least 30 minutes and up to 2 hours. Stored in a tightly sealed container, the syrup keeps for a week.

    • 1 cup sugar
    • 1/2 cup water
    • 1 tbsp dried lavender leaves

  6. Grenadine

    Combine the juice and sugar in a saucepan and place over medium-high heat. Stir frequently and bring to just under a boil. Reduce the heat to just under a simmer and cook for 20 to 30 minutes. When the syrup has darkemed and is thick enough to coat the back of a metal spoon, it's ready. Remove from the heat and let cool. Add the orange blossom water and stir. Label, date and refrigerate. It keeps for 4 weeks.

    • 2 cups POM pomegranate juice
    • 1 cup sugar
    • 2 dashes orange flower water

Old-Fashioned Cocktails:

  1. Old-Fashioned Whiskey Cocktail

    This is the basic formula for an Old-Fashioned, be it 1887 or 2017. Whether you reach for mellow beurbon or spicy rye is a matter of choice: both work wonderfully in the drink. If you're lacking a muddler, a bar spoon of simple syrup will do the job of the suger cube.


    Saturate a sugar cube with bitters and a bar spoon of warm water at the bottom of an Old-Fashioned glass. Muddle until the sugar dissolves. Add whiskey and stir. Add one large piece of ice and stir until chilled, about 30 seconds. Twist a piece of orange zest over the drink and drop into the glass.

    • 2 ounces bourbon or rye
    • 2 dashes Angostura bitters
    • 1 sugar cube
    • Orange twist

  2. Trinidad Old-Fashioned

    It takes a little bit of searching to find the ingredients for this simple but excellent drink. You can order the cider syrup from places in Vermont. The other two products you can find in boutique liqour stores.


    Stir all the ingredients, except the lemon and orange twists, together over ice in a mixing glass and strain into a large rocks glass or Double Old-Fashioned glass filled with one large ice cube. Twist a piece of lemon zest and a piece of orange zest over the drink and drop into the glass.

    • 2 ounces Plantation Trinidad Old Reserve Rum
    • 1 scant bar spoon Wood's Bioled Cider Syrup
    • 2 to 3 dashes St. Elizabeth's Allspice Dram
    • Lemon twist
    • Orange twist

  3. Wisconsin Old-Fashioned

    Ask for an Old-Fashioned in Wisconsin and you get a drink made with domestic brandy poured over the muddled pulp of sugar, an orange slice, and a maraschino cherry, and topped with soda pop or soda water.


    Add Angostura bitters into a glass. Add orange wedge and luxardo cherry, then muddle together. Add the simple syrup, brandy, and ice, then stir. In seperate Old-Fashioned glass, add one large ice cube, and strain the brandy mix. Twist a piece of orange zest over the drink and drop into the glass. If asked for sweet, add Sprite on top, if asked for sour, add Squirt on top.

    • 2 ounces brandy
    • 1/4 ounce Demerara simple syrup
    • 2 dashes Angostura bitters
    • Orange twist
    • Orange wedge

  4. Eau Clair Old-Fashioned

    Ask for an Old-Fashioned in Wisconsin and you get a drink made with domestic brandy poured over the muddled pulp of sugar, an orange slice, and a maraschino cherry, and topped with soda pop or soda water. This drink is a refined version of the same, with cognac stepping in for the local brandy, and the fruit mimicked by the cherry bitters and orange twist. If you can't find the Ferrand, substitute an equally dry quality brand.


    Combine the syrup and bitters in an Old-Fashioned glass. Add the cognac and stir. Add one large ice cube and stir until chilled, about 30 seconds. Twist a piece of orange zest over the drink and drop into the glass.

    • 2 ounces Pierre Ferrand 1840 cognac

    • 3 dashes Bttercube Cherry Bark Vanilla bitters
    • Orange twist

Improved Cocktails:

  1. Martini

    Like everybody else, I have my own ideas of what makes a great Martini. Two to one or three to one suits me, proportion-wise. It hits the sweet spot between dry and wet. I like a number of gins for this recipe, depending on my mood: Beefeater if I want a more assertive drink; Plymouth if I want a more elegant one; Bombay (not Sapphire) if I want something down the middle. If you can find the higher-proof Gordon's that is available overseas and in Duty Free shops, that makes a good, traditional drink, too. Dolin dry vermouth is a good choice for all. For a garnish, the lemon twist is the natural choice to me, as it marries well with the botanical components of many London dry gins.


    Combine all the ingredients except the lemon twist in a mixing glass filled with ice and stir until chilled, about 30 seconds. Strain into a chilled coupe. Express a lemon twist over the drink and drop into the glass.

    • 2 ounces gin
    • 1 ounce dry vermouth
    • 2-3 dashes orange bitters
    • Lemon twist

  2. Humblebrag

    Combine all the ingredients except the grapefruit twist in a mixing glass filled with ice and stir until chilled, about 30 seconds. Strain into a chilled coupe. Express a grapefruit twist over the drink and drop into the glass.

    • 2 ounces Hendrick's gin
    • 1 ounce dry vermouth
    • 2-3 dashes grapefruit bitters
    • Grapefruit twist

  3. Vodka Martini

    I would err on the side of dry and clean by using less vermouth so that it exerts only a small influence on the cocktail, keeping the vodka front and center. Absolut Elyx makes the best choice for vodka.


    Stir all the ingredients over ice, then strain into a chilled coupe. Garnish with the olive.

    • 2 1/2 ounces vodka
    • 3/4 ounce dry vermouth
    • 1 olive

  4. Espresso Martini

    If you’re looking for a pick-me-up post-dinner, the Espresso Martini is here to jolt you awake with its boozy magical powers. The cocktail has been jumpstarting outings and extending nights ever since making its debut in the 1980s. If you have an espresso machine, you can pull a shot and put it in the freezer for a quick chill. That will keep the hot liquid from melting the ice and overly diluting the drink. If you don’t have an espresso machine, using cold brew concentrate is an easy hack.


    Add vodka, coffee liqueur, espresso and simple syrup to a shaker filled with ice and shake until well-chilled. Strain into a chilled cocktail glass. Garnish with 3 coffee beans.

    • 2 ounces vodka
    • 1/2 ounce coffee liqueur (usually Kahlúa)
    • 1 ounce espresso, freshly brewed (or cold brew concentrate)
    • 1/2 ounce simple syrup (1:1)
    • Coffee beans
    • 1/2 ounce chocolate syrup (mocha espresso)
    • 1 drop of peppermint essential (peppermint espresso)

  5. Pornstar Martini

  13. Manhattan

    For this drink, rye is the preferred spirt among most mixologists and the younger set. Its rough edges lend the cocktail added structure. But bourbon has a case to plead. Some choose to split the base spirit between bourbon and rye. And then there's the matter of which brand of vermouth to pair the whiskey with. A home bartender can have a lot of fun within this simple formula. Experiment.


    Combine all of the ingredients except the brandied cherry in a mixing glass filled with ice and stir until chilled, about 30 seconds. Strain into a chilled coupe. Garnish with the brandied cherry.

    • 2 ounces bourbon or rye whiskey
    • 1 ounce sweet vermouth

    • Brandied cherry

  19. Revolver

    This drink was created by San Francisco bartneder Jon Santer, partly in reponse to the arrival on the market of Bulleit bourbon. The theatrical employment of a flamed orange twist made it a much-ordered drink for a time.

Sours:

  1. Tom Collins

    The Tom Collins had one of the most lively beginnings in cocktail history and has continued to endure. It helps that it has hearty roots leading all the way back to England, where it was called John Collins and came in punch forms. It also helps that it tastes great. With the introduction of Old Tom gin, Tom Collins once again had excitement. For some reason, London dry, which makes a Martini stand up straight and packs a punch into a Gin and Tonic, renders a Tom Collins thin and uninteresting. But it makes a Tom Collins rich and flavorful. Hayman's is a fine choice of Old Tom gin, but many work well.


    Shake the gin, syrup, and juice with ice until chilled, about 15 seconds. Strain into an ice-filled highball and top with soda water, about 1 once. If desired, garnish with a lemon wedge and cherry flag.

    • 2 ounces Old Tom gin
    • 1 ounce simple syrup (1:1)
    • 3/4 ounce lemon juice
    • Soda water
    • Lemon wedge and brandied cherry (optional)

  2. Gin Fizz

    The difference between a fizz and a collins is that a fizz is not served "long" and comes without ice. But, in this case, it's a difference with a distinction. One is a quick fix, the other is a drink to linger over. Both have their time and place. Add an egg white to the shaker and you have a Silver Gin Fizz; and egg yolk, and it's a Golden Fizz.


    Combine all the ingredients except the soda water with ice in a cocktail shaker and shake vigorously until chilled and frothy, about 15 seconds. Strain into a rocks glass. Top with soda water, about 1 ounce.

    • 2 ounces gin
    • 1 ounce simple syrup (1:1)
    • 3/4 ounce lemon juice
    • Soda water

A Boston shaker

Equipment

This two-piece shaker, composed of a standard mixing glass and a metal mixing tin, is suitable for stirred drinks (for which you only need the mixing glass) and shaken drinks (for which you require both parts).

A bar spoon

Equipment

A long bar spoon (approximately 11 inches) is need for all stirred drinks.

A jigger

Equipment

Most jiggers are stainless steel and have a dual-measure design. The most common ones have a 1-ounce capacity on one end and a 1/2 ounce capacity on the other; or a 1 1/2-, 3/4-ounce setup. It's good to have of each to ensure accurate measurements.

Strainers

Equipment

You need a julep strainer (which has a perforated bowl shape) for stirred drinks and a Hawthorne strainer (which has a metal coil wrapped around its bowl to keep out citrus pulp) for shaken drinks. The Hawthorne can do double duty if needed.

A muddler

Equipment

You need a muddler for certain drinks that require you to mash up fruits, herbs, or sugar cubes at the bottom of a mixing glass or serving glass. Old-fashioned wooden specimens work the best.

Large-scale ice trays

Equipment

A large ice cube makes a big difference, both aesthetically and tastewise, in some stirred, sipping drinks, like the Old-Fashioned. Molds for these sorts of cubes, typically 2 by 2 inches in size, are now very easy to find.
